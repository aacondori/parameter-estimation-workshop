# Phase Two: Extended Research Projects

**For Continuing Students**

---

## Overview

Phase Two extends the three-day intensive into a semester-long research experience. Selected student pairs dive deeper into the mathematics of parameter estimation, exploring questions where models meet messy reality and where elegant techniques reveal surprising connections.

Each pair tackles one research project—some computational, some theoretical, some wrestling with real experimental puzzles. All are designed to feel like authentic research: you will encounter genuine obstacles, develop your own approaches, and produce results that even your mentors may not fully anticipate.

**Duration:** Fall semester (September - mid-November)  
**Format:** Virtual mentorship + monthly in-person meetings  
**Culmination:** Research symposium at university campus

---

## Research Projects

Each pair selects one project that matches their interests and strengths. Projects span theory, computation, experimental design, and data analysis—all rooted in the physics of cooling but reaching toward broader questions about models, measurements, and the nature of scientific inference.

---

### Project 1 — When the Environment Won't Cooperate

Real experiments rarely happen in perfectly controlled conditions. In this project you will investigate what happens to parameter estimation when the environment itself is changing — and whether you can detect that change from the object's temperature data alone.

**What you'll explore:**  
Models that adapt to shifting conditions • Detective work with imperfect data • The boundary between what's knowable and what's not

---

### Project 2 — The Inside Story

A standard model assumes every part of an object is at the same temperature at the same moment. For a thick object, that is simply not true. This project asks: how badly does the simple model fail, and can a richer model that tracks both surface and interior recover what the simpler one misses?

**What you'll explore:**  
When simple models break • Building complexity into mathematics • Validation against reality

---

### Project 3 — One Trick, Many Models

A clever technique developed for one specific problem turns out to apply much more broadly. This project transplants that technique into a completely different scientific context — population growth — and investigates both when it works beautifully and when it breaks down.

**What you'll explore:**  
Mathematical pattern recognition • Transferring insight across domains • Limits of analogy

---

### Project 4 — More Parameters, Better Science?

A more flexible model will almost always fit data better. But does a better fit mean better science? This project confronts the tension between complexity and reliability, and develops principled tools for deciding when a more elaborate model is genuinely justified.

**What you'll explore:**  
The art of model selection • Statistical rigor meets scientific judgment • When less is more

---

### Project 5 — Does Timing Matter?

When you collect measurements matters — not just how many you collect. This project investigates how the timing of observations shapes whether a key parameter can be reliably recovered at all, and develops practical guidelines for designing better experiments.

**What you'll explore:**  
Experimental design as mathematics • Information content of measurements • Strategic data collection

---

### Project 6 — How Much Should You Trust Your Answer?

Parameter estimation produces a single best answer, but that answer is only as reliable as the data behind it. This project uses a simulation-based technique to make uncertainty visible — revealing not just what the best answer is, but how much it could plausibly vary if the experiment were repeated.

**What you'll explore:**  
Computational approaches to uncertainty • Visualizing what you don't know • Honest claims from imperfect data

---

### Project 7 — When the Model and Reality Disagree

Real data from a real experiment refuses to behave the way a textbook model predicts — roughly half the time, in fact. This project investigates why, proposes competing physical explanations, weighs the evidence for each, and designs an experiment that could settle the question.

**What you'll explore:**  
Scientific hypothesis testing • Model criticism and refinement • From puzzle to research proposal

## Program Structure

### September: Project Selection and Setup
- **Week 1:** Orientation meeting, introduction to all seven projects
- **Week 2:** Conversations with mentors, project matching process
- **Week 3:** Literature review, background reading
- **Week 4:** Initial planning and scope definition

### October: Core Research Phase
- **Weekly:** Virtual mentor meetings (1 hour)
- **Mid-month:** In-person workshop at university
- **Milestones:**
  - Complete theoretical background study
  - Implement basic algorithms
  - Generate preliminary results

### November: Analysis, Refinement, and Symposium
- **Weekly:** Progress updates and troubleshooting
- **Early month:** Finalize computational experiments
- **Week 2:** Draft presentations and deliverables
- **Week 3:** **Research Symposium** (see [symposium details](phase2-event.md))
- **Week 4:** Final revisions and submission

**Program concludes:** Mid-to-late November

## What You'll Develop (Phase Two)

### Mathematical Depth
- Prove mathematical theorems or develop rigorous computational arguments
- Read and critically evaluate research literature
- Derive formulas and construct logical chains of reasoning
- Identify when intuition misleads and when it guides

### Computational Sophistication
- Implement algorithms from scratch, understanding every step
- Debug systematically when results don't make sense
- Create visualizations that reveal rather than obscure
- Write code that others (including future you) can understand and use

### Research Mindset
- Formulate questions worth pursuing
- Distinguish between roadblocks and dead ends
- Persist through confusion without losing direction
- Recognize when to seek help and what help to seek

### Scientific Communication
- Write about mathematics with clarity and precision
- Present ideas to audiences with different backgrounds
- Give and receive constructive criticism gracefully
- Collaborate remotely with discipline and mutual respect

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I participate if I'm not planning to major in math?**  
A: Absolutely! Phase Two students have gone on to major in engineering, computer science, physics, economics, and other fields where mathematical modeling is valuable.

**Q: How are projects assigned to pairs?**  
A: After acceptance, you'll have conversations with mentors about your interests and strengths. Project assignments balance student preferences with program capacity and mentor expertise.

**Q: What mathematical background do I need?**  
A: If you succeeded in Phase One, you have the foundation. Each project builds on Calculus I and strong algebra, introducing additional concepts as needed through readings and mentor guidance.

**Q: What if we get stuck?**  
A: That's expected! Your mentors are there to help you work through challenges. Getting unstuck is part of the research process — and often where the deepest learning happens.
